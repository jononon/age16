Industry

Strip Mining, Hazelton, PA, photograph, accessed May 25, 2018, https://www.andrew.cmu.edu/user/ct0u/haz_coal_stripping_1910-300w.jpg.

Hazelton was founded in 1851 as a coal mining town. As the anthracite coal mining boomed, the population of the town boomed with an influx of eastern European immigrants. My great-grandfather was one such immigrant. He died in a coalmining accident in 1946: incidents like this were all too common.
By the time my father was 16 (1978), coal mining was in sharp decline and had fallen off significantly from it's 50s peak. Both the town's population and economic conditions were trending downwards as coal production moved from deep mines to heavially automated open strip mining. Open, scathed faces of mountains now surround the city.

Recreation

Hazelton PA Laurel Theater Advertisement 1957, photograph, driveins.com, accessed May 25, 2018, http://drive-ins.com/imagesdi/pa/patlaur018.jpg.

"I didn’t watch too much television but we did go to the movies often. Superman, Grease, Animal House and Jaws were big hits. In the summer, we would go to drive in movie theaters which were super fun. Information was limited to newspapers, magazines, terrestrial radio and television. There were only four TV stations ABC, CBS, NBC and PBS. We didn’t think too much about the larger world around us. We spent our free time with family and friends. We listened to the radio and the music was amazing! The Clash, Police, Elvis Costello, Cars, Talking Heads, Springsteen, and Steely Dan were all incredibly popular."

Christopher Damico on entertainment and media during 1978.
During the winter, many took to the surrounding pocono mountains to ski and snowboard. Summertime activities included fishing and hunting. Some families had summer homes on nearby lakes and rivers. When times were good, many families visited local amusement parks. One such park, Knoebels, has remained America's largest free-admission park for 90 years.

Home Life

My family lived in a home two blocks from the center of town. There was both a vibrant Jewish community and an active Catholic community. My grandfather operated his dentistry practice out of the first and second floors of 55 N Laurel St. and my family lived on the third floor. This meant that despite the fact that there was a candy shop next door, my grandfather would rarley let his kids eat much candy.
Hazelton was safe, it seemed vibrant despite declining population, and it was relativley strong.

Work / Energy Crisis

When my dad was in high school, he worked for a Ford dealership in Hazelton washing, delivering, and transporting cars. My dad happened to be 16 during the oil crisis and saw a decline in demand for American cars firsthand.
